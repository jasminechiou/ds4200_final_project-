About this page

This page explores the abundance and nativeness of species in California's National Parks, allowing us to explore how this plays into biodiversity and the preservation of these parks' ecosystems.

Abundance and Nativeness

This interactive heatmap shows use the species count data categorized by abundance and nativeness between the three parks. Users can toggle between radio buttons to show the species count, abundance, and nativeness for each park. The x-axis represents the abundance categories, while the y-axis represents nativeness. The blue color gradient indicates the number of species corresponding to each combination of abundance and nativeness, allowing users to easily identify areas with high or low species counts. This intersection of abundance and nativeness provides a detailed, categorical view of species distribution, offering valuable insights for biodiversity analysis. By highlighting patterns in species distribution, the heatmap helps inform conservation and management efforts, particularly in areas where invasive species may be dominating. Invasive species often display irregular abundance patterns, such as appearing suddenly in previously underrepresented categories, and this visualization can act as an early warning system for such disruptions. By focusing on anomalies or unexplained increases in abundance, park managers and ecologists can prioritize further investigation and develop targeted strategies to control invasive species before they spread, supporting ecosystem preservation.

A Closer Look

This Sankey Diagram takes a closer look at abundance and nativeness by zooming into which orders are both abundant and non-native. Orders are one level of the Linnaeus classification system that is used to classify animals into a hierarchical tree. The Sankey Diagram looks at these orders and connects them to which category they are in, as well as which park they come from (these are the nodes). This plot is also interactive- the user can click nodes to highlight the connected edges, hover over the nodes to get more information, and even drag around the nodes to better visualize whatever aspect they want to dig deeper into. What we can learn from this visualization is that most of the non-native and abundant/common species are Vascular Plants, specifically those from the Poales order. Poales are flowering plants and includes plants such as various grasses. This tells us that if these national parks want to limit any invasive species, this is one place they could start. This could be especially important because Poales can have negative effects on wildlife, water cycles, and the economy by disrupting agriculture.
